Geological context
The unit sampled is a variably porphyritic metamonzogranite assigned to the Esperance Supersuite (Smithies et al., 2015).
The 1198–1140 Ma emplacement of the Esperance Supersuite coincided with Stage II of the Albany–Fraser Orogeny
(Spaggiari et al., 2014). Intrusions of the Esperance Supersuite are generally lower in metamorphic grade and less
pervasively deformed than those of the Recherche Supersuite, which was emplaced during Stage I (1330–1260 Ma) of the
Albany–Fraser Orogeny (Spaggiari et al., 2014; Smithies et al., 2015). Pidgeon (1990) reported multi-grain and single-grain
U–Pb zircon intercept dates obtained by isotope dilution thermal ionization mass spectrometry (IDTIMS). A sample of
porphyritic biotite monzogranite, collected about 8.3 km to the south, yielded an igneous crystallization age of 1174 ± 12
Ma (sample W139, Pidgeon, 1990). About 11.0 km to the southeast, a granoblastic, orthopyroxene-bearing granodiorite
gneiss provided an igneous crystallization age of 1289 ± 10 Ma (sample W141, Pidgeon, 1990).

Petrographic description
This sample is a coarse-grained, inequigranular biotite–hornblende metamonzogranite (Fig. 2), consisting of about 40% K-
feldspar, 25% plagioclase, 20% quartz, 7% biotite, 4% hornblende, 2% myrmekite, <1% titanite, <1% allanite, and trace
apatite, chlorite, pyrite, magnetite and zircon. K-feldspar is microperthitic and forms anhedral, equant to slightly tabular
phenocrysts up to 12 mm in diameter, and more numerous grains up to 6 mm diameter. Many K-feldspar grains exhibit
microcline twinning, some contain inclusions of other minerals, and some have lobes of myrmekite up to 1.5 mm diameter
along their margins. Plagioclase forms anhedral to subhedral, tabular grains up to 4.5 mm long that are partly replaced by
sericite, and rare, albite-rimmed inclusions are present in K-feldspar. Interstitial quartz is anhedral, irregular, up to 5 mm in
                                                                1
size, and weakly to moderately strained. Brown biotite forms subhedral laths up to 2 mm long, partly in decussate
aggregates with hornblende, titanite and allanite, and exhibits minor alteration to chlorite. Green hornblende is subhedral,
equant, up to 2.5 mm in size, and contains very rare magnetite inclusions that are mostly replaced by pyrite. Irregular
anhedral grains of pink titanite are up to 1 mm in size and associated with biotite and/or hornblende. Subhedral to euhedral
green allanite crystals are metamict and up to 1.5 mm in size, and apatite is subhedral to euhedral, tabular, and up to 0.15
mm long. The sample lacks a tectonic foliation but contains evidence of deformation in the form of microcline twinning in
K-feldspar and strain in quartz. These features and the presence of conspicuous myrmekite suggest that deformation
occurred at low to moderate metamorphic grade.

Zircon morphology
Zircons isolated from this sample are colourless to dark brown or opaque and mainly euhedral. The crystals are up to 350
µm long, and elongate, with aspect ratios up to 8:1. In cathodoluminescence (CL) images, most crystals exhibit concentric
zoning, and many contain low-uranium cores. A CL image of representative zircons is shown in Figure 3.

Analytical details
This sample was analysed on 13–14 January 2022 using SHRIMP-B. Twelve analyses of the M257 standard were obtained
during the session, of which nine analyses were assigned the minimum external spot-to-spot (reproducibility) uncertainty of
0.50% (1σ) and indicated a 238U/206Pb* calibration uncertainty of 0.23% (1σ). Isotopic mass fractionation of 207Pb/206Pb
ratios during the session was corrected by reference to the OGC1 standard; measured ratios were decreased by 0.08%.
Calibration uncertainties are included in the errors of 238U/206Pb* ratios and dates listed in Table 1 (provided in the links
section). Common-Pb corrections were applied to all analyses using contemporaneous isotopic compositions determined
according to the model of Stacey and Kramers (1975).

Interpretation
The analyses are concordant to moderately discordant (Fig. 4). Four analyses are >5% discordant. The dates obtained from
these four analyses (Group D; Table 1) are unreliable, and are considered not to be geologically significant. The remaining
22 analyses can be divided into two groups, based on their 207Pb*/206Pb* and 238U/206Pb* ratios and their U and Th
concentrations.
Group I comprises 21 analyses of 19 zircons (Table 1), which yield a concordia age of 1167 ± 5 Ma (MSWD = 1.5). These
analyses indicate mainly moderate U concentrations (62–1618 ppm, median 248 ppm) and moderate Th concentrations
(33–792 ppm, median 171 ppm).

Group P comprises one analysis (Table 1), which yields a 238U/206Pb* date of 1108 ± 16 Ma (1σ). This analysis indicates
very high U concentration (2520 ppm) and very high Th concentration (2029 ppm).
The date of 1167 ± 5 Ma for the 21 analyses in Group I is interpreted as the magmatic crystallization age of the
monzogranite. No significant or systematic difference in apparent age could be discerned between low-U zircon cores and
high-U outer zones. The date of 1108 ± 16 Ma (1σ) for the single analysis in Group P is interpreted to reflect minor loss of
radiogenic Pb.
