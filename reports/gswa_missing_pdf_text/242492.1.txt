                         242492.1: migmatitic gneiss, Reserve Well
                              (Annean Supersuite; Youanmi Terrane, Yilgarn Craton)
                                 SS Romano, MTD Wingate, Y LU, IOH Fielding and TJ Ivanic


Location and sampling
MURGOO SG50-14, MURGOO 2143                                                        Zone 50, MGA 441558E 7003242N
Warox Site TJIMUR190001                                                               Sampled on 3 September 2019
This sample was collected near the western edge of a 200 m-wide outcrop east of the Beringarra–Pindar Road, about 5.4
km east-northeast of Mount Nicolay, 4.6 km west of Little Well, and 1.6 km south-southeast of Reserve Well.

Geological context
The unit sampled is a migmatitic granitic gneiss (Fig. 1) assigned to the Annean Supersuite (Van Kranendonk et al. 2013).
The 2825–2733 Ma Annean Supersuite consists of metagranitic rocks, commonly foliated, and weakly metamorphosed
layered mafic–ultramafic intrusions (Ivanic et al., 2012). At this locality, the granitic gneiss is a stromatic migmatite and is
crosscut by a 0.5 m-wide dyke of metagranodiorite that yielded an igneous crystallization age of 2687 ± 4 Ma (GSWA
242493, Romano et al., 2023c). Samples of metamonzogranite collected about 49 and 67 km to the east-northeast yielded
igneous crystallization ages of 2701 ± 5 Ma (GSWA 242491, Romano et al., 2023b) and 2693 ± 7 Ma (GSWA 242490,
Romano et al., 2023a), respectively.

Petrographic description
This sample is a medium-grained migmatitic gneiss (Fig. 2), consisting of about 35–40% plagioclase, 30–35% quartz, 20%
K-feldspar, 7–8% biotite, 2% magnetite, and trace allanite, titanite, apatite and zircon. Plagioclase exhibits minor sericite
alteration, and forms subhedral, equant to tabular grains up to 6 mm long, and anhedral, equant grains up to 1 mm in size
within polygonal recrystallized aggregates. Quartz is anhedral, irregular, weakly to moderately strained, and up to 10 mm in
size, although mostly less than 5 mm. Mutual quartz boundaries are finely intergrown, and boundaries with adjacent
feldspar grains are lobate. K-feldspar is anhedral, equant, microperthitic and up to 5 mm in size. Some K-feldspar exhibits
well-developed microcline twinning, and larger grains contain inclusions of plagioclase, quartz and biotite. The polygonal
aggregates consist mainly of plagioclase and K-feldspar with minor quartz, biotite, magnetite and myrmekite. Brown biotite
is randomly oriented and forms slender subhedral laths up to 2 mm long. Magnetite is subhedral, pitted, very finely

fractured and up to 1.5 mm in diameter. Allanite is subhedral and up to 0.7 mm in size, and titanite is anhedral, irregular and
up to 0.5 mm across. Subhedral apatite is up to 0.4 mm long. Despite evidence of deformation and recrystallization at
moderate metamorphic grade, this sample in thin section does not exhibit a penetrative foliation or compositional layering.

Zircon morphology
Zircons isolated from this sample are pale brown to dark brown or opaque, mainly subhedral, and slightly rounded. The
crystals are up to 400 µm long, and mainly elongate, with aspect ratios up to 7:1. In cathodoluminescence (CL) images,
concentric zoning is ubiquitous, and the cores of some crystals are lower in uranium than the outer zones. A CL image of
representative zircons is shown in Figure 3.

Analytical details
This sample was analysed during two sessions on 17–18 September 2021, using SHRIMP-B. Analyses 1.1 to 6.1 (spot
numbers 1–8) were obtained during the first session, together with five analyses of the M257 standard, of which four
analyses indicated an external spot-to-spot (reproducibility) uncertainty of 0.50% (1σ) and a 238U/206Pb* calibration
uncertainty of 0.76% (1σ). Analyses 7.1 to 15.1 (spot numbers 9–21) were obtained during the second session, together
with seven analyses of the M257 standard, of which five analyses indicated an external spot-to-spot (reproducibility)
uncertainty of 0.64% (1σ) and a 238U/206Pb* calibration uncertainty of 0.37% (1σ). Isotopic mass fractionation of
207Pb/206Pb ratios during both sessions was corrected by reference to the OGC1 standard; measured ratios were increased
by 0.35%. Calibration uncertainties are included in the errors of 238U/206Pb* ratios and dates listed in Table 1 (provided in
the Links section). Common-Pb corrections were applied to all analyses using contemporaneous isotopic compositions
determined according to the model of Stacey and Kramers (1975).

Results
Twenty-one analyses were obtained from 15 zircons. Results are listed in Table 1, and shown in a concordia diagram (Fig.
4).

Interpretation
The analyses are concordant to moderately discordant (Fig. 4), and form two groups, based on their 207Pb*/206Pb* and
Th/U ratios, U concentrations, and positions within the crystals.

Group I comprises 15 analyses of 13 zircon cores and rims (Table 1), which yield a weighted mean 207Pb*/206Pb* date of
2741 ± 4 Ma (MSWD = 0.71). These analyses indicate mostly moderate U concentrations (99–763 ppm, median 261 ppm)
and low to moderate Th/U ratios (0.1 – 1.3, median 0.5).

Group P comprises six analyses of six zircon rims (Table 1), which yield 207Pb*/206Pb* dates of 2713–2665 Ma. These
                                                              2
analyses indicate high U concentrations (752–1100 ppm, median 857 ppm) and low Th/U ratios (0.05 – 0.11, median 0.06).
The date of 2741 ± 4 Ma for the 15 analyses in Group I is interpreted as the igneous crystallization age. The dates of 2713–
2665 Ma for the six analyses in Group P are interpreted to reflect ancient loss of radiogenic Pb.
