                        229320.1: metamonzogranite, Rosary Bore
                 (Walganna Suite, Bald Rock Supersuite; Youanmi Terrane, Yilgarn Craton)
                                SS Romano, Y Lu, IOH Fielding, MTD Wingate and TJ Ivanic


Location and sampling
CUE SG50-15, DALGARANGA 2342                                                      Zone 50, MGA 513752E 6928483N
Warox Site TJIDAL180102                                                                    Sampled on 29 April 2018
This sample was collected from a whaleback ridge, about 12.2 km east of Dalgaranga, 8.2 km north-northeast of
Dalgaranga Hill, and 2.2 km south-southwest of Rosary Bore.

Geological context
The unit sampled is a metamonzogranite of the Walganna Suite of the Bald Rock Supersuite (Van Kranendonk et al.,
2013). Granitic rocks of the 2650–2600 Ma Walganna Suite are among the youngest in the Youanmi Terrane, and consists
of biotite ± muscovite monzogranites to syenogranites that range in texture from coarsely porphyritic K-feldspar to coarse-
grained, equigranular rocks (Van Kranendonk et al., 2013). At this locality, the metamonzogranite contains mafic clots and
xenoliths of amphibolite (Fig. 1). The mafic clots containing biotite and amphiboles are typically elongated and aligned in
north–south direction. The metamonzogranite intruded at the northwestern flank of the Dalgaranga greenstone belt.
Although this locality was originally mapped as the 2787–2733 Ma Cullculli Suite of the Annean Supersuite, the
geochronology results provided below indicate it is Walganna Suite of the Bald Rock Supersuite. A metamonzogranite of
the Big Bell Suite, sampled about 5.3 km to the north, yielded an igneous crystallization age of 2709 ± 7 Ma (GSWA
238531, Wingate et al., 2019).

Petrographic description
The sample is a medium-grained, slightly inequigranular biotite metamonzogranite, consisting of about 30–35% quartz,
30% K-feldspar, 25–30% plagioclase, 10% biotite, and accessory magnetite, apatite and zircon. The sample has undergone
moderate alteration by clay–sericite after plagioclase and chlorite after biotite. Quartz occurs as anhedral grains to 3.5 mm
in size, with undulating grain boundaries. K-feldspar is perthitic, and occurs as anhedral to subhedral grains up to 5 mm in
size, with some grains containing small inclusions of quartz, plagioclase and biotite. Many of the larger K-feldspar grains
have simple twinning, or poorly to well-developed microcline twinning, and a few grains are mantled by myrmekite
                                                              1
occurring as round lobes up to 0.3 mm in size. Plagioclase occurs as subhedral, stubby to tabular laths up to 2 mm in
length. Sericite/clay alteration reflects a poorly developed compositional zoning in some grains. Biotite occurs as subhedral
prismatic grains that are mostly <1 mm in length. Magnetite is subhedral to anhedral, up to 0.8 mm in diameter. Apatite
occurs as euhedral grains to 0.25 mm in length, and zircon is abundant, and marked by dark radiation halos in biotite.
Although the sample lacks a tectonic fabric, it has undergone mid-crustal deformation evidenced by strain, microcline
twinning and minor myrmekite.

Zircon morphology
Zircons isolated from this sample are colourless to pink and dark brown and mainly euhedral to subhedral. Many zircons
contain small inclusions. The crystals are up to 400 µm long, and elongate, with aspect ratios up to 7:1. In
cathodoluminescence (CL) images, concentric zoning is ubiquitous, and many crystals contain high-U rims that are too
narrow to be analysed. A CL image of representative zircons is shown in Figure 2.

Analytical details
This sample was analysed on 21–22 January 2022, using SHRIMP-B. Nine analyses of the M257 standard obtained during
the session indicated an external spot-to-spot (reproducibility) uncertainty of 0.5% (1σ) and a 238U/206Pb* calibration
uncertainty of 0.22% (1σ). Calibration uncertainties are included in the errors of 238U/206Pb* ratios and dates listed in
Table 1 (provided in the Links section). Common-Pb corrections were applied to all analyses using contemporaneous
isotopic compositions determined according to the model of Stacey and Kramers (1975).

Results
Seventeen analyses were obtained from 17 zircons. Results are listed in Table 1 and shown in a concordia diagram (Fig. 3).

Interpretation
The analyses are concordant to moderately discordant (Fig. 3), and form a single group based on their 207Pb*/206Pb*
                                                              2
ratios.

Group I comprises 17 analyses (Table 1), which yield a weighted mean 207Pb*/206Pb* date of 2630 ± 7 Ma (MSWD =
0.95).
The date of 2630 ± 7 Ma for the 17 analyses in Group I is interpreted as the igneous crystallization age of the monzogranite
protolith.
